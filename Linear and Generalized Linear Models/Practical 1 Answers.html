Exercise 1: Weight gain of chickens

A researcher conducts an experiment to examine the relationship between the weight gain of chickens whose diets had been supplemented by different amounts of amino acid lysine and the amount of lysine ingested. Since the percentage of lysine is known, and we can monitor the amount of feed consumed, we can determine the amount of lysine eaten. A random sample of twelve 2-week-old chickens was selected for the study. Each was caged separately and was allowed to eat at will from feed composed of a base supplemented with lysine.

First we will introduce the data into R and make a plot to visualize it.

# weight gain y of chickens with amount of lysine x ingested
y <- c(14.7, 17.8, 19.6, 18.4, 20.5, 21.1, 17.2, 18.7, 20.2, 16.0, 17.8, 19.4)
x <- c(0.09, 0.14, 0.18, 0.15, 0.16, 0.23, 0.11, 0.19, 0.23, 0.13, 0.17, 0.21)

# plot the data
plot(x, y)
  1. Take a good look at the scatterplot of x versus y. Give an estimate of the correlation coefficient between x and y. Explain the sign and give and estimate of the magnitude.


  1. There is a positive trend in the plot. If x increases y increases as well. This means the correlation coefficient has a positive (+) sign. The magnitude is a bit more difficult to estimate by eye, but given that the data points lie reasonably close to an imaginary line, I estimate the correlation coefficient to be around 0.8 which is reasonably close to the actual value of 0.85.

Next we will calculate the correlation coefficient.

cor(x, y)
[1] 0.8521698
  1. Compare the calculated correlation coefficient estimate with your own estimate from the previous exercise. Was your estimate close? what about the sign?


  1. Depending on your estimate your value might be close or somewhat further away from the calculated value. Your estimated sign should positive as the trend is clearly positive.

Correlation coefficients describe the linear association between two variables. With linear regression we want to model this relationship. This is what we will do next. Exercise 2: NO2 emissions in Norway

The data are a subsample of 500 observations from a study where air pollution at a road is related to traffic volume and meteorological variables, collected by the Norwegian Public Roads Administration. The response variable (column 1) consist of hourly values of the logarithm of the concentration of nitrogen dioxide (NO2) particles, measured at Alnabru in Oslo, Norway, between November and May in two consecutive years, starting 2001. The predictor variables (columns 2 to 8) are:

  • x1: the logarithm of the number of cars per hour
  • x2: temperature at two meters above ground (C)
  • x3: wind speed (meters/second)
  • x4: the temperature difference between 25 and 2 m above ground (C)
  • x5: wind direction (0-360 degrees)
  • x6: hour of the day
  • x7: day number from October 1, 2001.

The data are available in the file Norway_NO2.csv. The data can be loaded by running the following line of code:

## Load data
Norway_NO2 <- read.csv("Norway_NO2.csv")

For a quick assessment of correlation between the outcome and the individual predictors we can make a matrix-scatterplot. With the following code, we make such scatterplots between y, x1, x2, x3 and y, x4, x5, x6, x7:

## Matrix of scatterplots
pairs(Norway_NO2[,1:4])

pairs(Norway_NO2[,c(1,5,6,7,8)])

  1. Which variables seem to have predictive value for NO2 emissions?


  1. Based on the plots, variable x1, x3 and x6 seem to have a relation with NO2.

  1. Give the percentage of variation explained by the model. Based on this value, do you expect that the model has predictive value?
  2. Give the value of the F test and its corresponding p-value.
  3. What is the null hypothesis of this test? And what is the alternative hypothesis of this test? Also give your conclusion based on this test.
  4. Can you give the estimated regression equation?
  5. What is the value and the meaning of the intercept?
  6. Can you test if wind speed has any predictive value (after correction for the effects of all other factors).
  7. Compare the RSE, the estimated slope and the corresponding standard error for wind speed of the model with only wind speed (as obtained previously) and the model with all variables. What has changed and why?
  8. Which of the other independent variables show a significant association with the NO2 concentration?


  1. The adjusted R2 is 0.51. Hence, the combined predictors explain 51% of the variation in NO2 concentration. We then indeed expect some predictive value for this model.

  2. F = 74.96 with p < .0001.

  3. The null hypothesis of this test is: H0 : β1 = β2 = β3 = β4 = β5 = β6 = β7 = 0. The alternative hypothesis postulates that: At least one βj ≠ 0. Based on the associated p-value we reject H0. Hence, at least one of the explanatory variables has a slope that differs from zero.

  4. The estimated regression equations is:

E(y)=.57+.505×x1−.024×x2−.125×x3+.167×x4+.00078×x5–.020×x6+.00036×x7.

  1. The value of the intercept (.57) indicates the concentration of NO2 when all predictors are zero. It has no direct meaning in this context as 0-values for all predictors seems to be outside the scope of the observed data (see the scatterplot matrices above).

  2. The null hypothesis is H0 : β3 = 0. The alternative hypothesis is Ha : β3 ≠ 0. The t-test statistic β3/se(β3)) =  − 8.992. The distribution of the test statistic under the null hypothesis H0 is t492 (i.e., a t-distribution with n − p − 1 = 500 − 7 − 1 = 492 degrees of freedom). The probability of attaining, under the null hypothesis, a value for t (in the absolute sense) that is at least as big as the observed test statistic is  < 2 × 10−16. Hence, p < .0001. Our conclusion then is that, given the effect of the other predictors, the slope of wind speed is significantly different from zero. Or, given the effect of the other predictors, there is a relationship between wind speed and NO2 concentration.

  3. The RSE was .7097 and decreased to .526 since the additional variables also explained variation in the NO2 concentration. The slope changed from −.13848 (se = .01781) to −.1254828 (se = .0139549). The se is smaller mainly due to smaller RSE. The small change in slope can be due to correlation with other explanatory variables in the model.

  4. At an α of .05 all seven predictors show a significant association with our outcome variable. At an α of .01 six predictors show a significant association with our outcome variable.

It is always important to check the assumptions underlying a model. Hence, we are going to do so, by making some relevant plots:

## Residuals and fitted values
resi <- residuals(lmmulti)
pred <- fitted(lmmulti)

## Allowing two side-by-side plots
par(mfrow = c(1,2))

## Scatterplot of residual vs predicted values
plot(resi ~ pred)
abline(h = 0)
title ("Homoscedasticity check")

## Normal Q-Q plot
qqnorm(resi)
qqline(resi)

  1. What is your conclusion based on these plots?


  1. The variance seems reasonably stable over the predictions. hence, equal variance is an acceptable assumption. The bulk of the points fall on the line in the QQ-plot. Some deviations in the tails is acceptable. Hence, normality is also a reasonable assumption.
